# 3.4 Notes: Combinations and Permutations

### Counting Sequences
- A **permutation** is a possible rearrangement of objects
  - i.e. 6 permutations of letters a, b, c:
    - abc, acb, bac, bca, cab, cba
    - The amount of permutations is equal to $3 \cdot 2 \cdot 1$
- With $n$ elements, there are $n!$ possible permutations
- Example: How many 4-letter words can you make from letters $a$ through $g$ without repeating?
  - $7$ choices for first, $6$ choices for second, [...] $4$ choices for fourth, so $7 \cdot 6 \cdot 5 \cdot 4 = 840$ permutations.
- For the example above, we want a formula to calculate that: $P(n, k)$
  - Starting at $n$ and going until $k$ numbers have been multiplied
  - i.e. $P(10,4) = 10 \cdot 9 \cdot 8 \cdot 7$
    - Also can be written as $\frac{10!}{6!}$
- Theorem 3.4.6: The number of $k$ permutations of $n$ elements is $P(n, k) = \frac{n!}{(n-k)!}$
- Example: How many functions $f: {(L = 3)} \rightarrow {(L=8)}$ are injective?
  - **Injective**: cannot use an element of the codomain more than once
  - $P(8,3) = 8 \cdot 7 \cdot 6$

### Quotient Principle
- $P(n, k) = \binom{n}{k} \cdot k! \rightarrow \binom{n}{k} = \frac{P(n, k)}{k!}$
- $\binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{(n-k)!k!}$

   - $P(n, k)$ is the number of $k$ permutations of $n$ elements 
   - In this problem, $P(11, 3)$ is the formula for the number of 3-vertex permutations that we can make with the 11 vertices in the screenshot
   - $P(11, 3) = \frac{11!}{(11-3)!} = \frac{11!}{8!} = 990$ permutations
   - In those $990$ permutations, many of those are flat on one axis (i.e. all 3 points are on the y-axis or all 3 on x-axis), so we must subtract those
   - $P(5,3) = \frac{5!}{2!} = 60$ possible permutations of points that are all on the y-axis
   - $P(7,3) = \frac{7!}{4!} = 210$ possible permutations of points that are only on the x-axis
   - $990 - 60 - 210 = 720$ possible permutations of 3 points that form a triangle
   - However, lots of these permutations will be the same but in a different order
   - There are a total of $6$ possible permutations of 3 items (in this case vertices), so we divide by 6
   - $\frac{720}{6} = 120$ possible triangles
